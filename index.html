Abstract

Content teams managing large libraries need a repeatable way to decide which pages deserve editorial review first, and this project asks whether a learned model can prioritize that queue better than a hand-written rule. It works from a 30,000-item, 44-feature anonymized snapshot of content performance and search-visibility signals drawn from the FlyRank internship dataset. A transparent rule-based baseline (staleness, visibility, click-through rate) was built first, then a Random Forest classifier was trained to reproduce and generalize that rule, validated under both a random split and a client-grouped split to check for leakage. Both splits agreed exactly, and the model recovered the rule's own weighting almost perfectly — staleness alone driving roughly 87% of its decisions — though the reported 1.00 accuracy reflects rule-recovery rather than an independent prediction, a limitation addressed directly in §5. The output is a ranked, reason-coded action queue built strictly for human-reviewed content prioritization, not automated publishing decisions.

1 Introduction

The decision. This work supports a single, concrete decision: which content pages should be prioritized for review and potential refresh, based on their search performance, engagement, and content freshness. It does not decide what to change on a page — only which pages are worth a human's time to look at first.

A concrete scenario. Picture a content manager who owns 30,000 pages and forty minutes before her next planning meeting. Scrolling the raw analytics dashboard is not an option — she needs a short, ranked list she can trust, each item labeled with why it's on the list. That is the exact gap this project fills: a queue, not a dashboard.

Who acts on it. The ranked recommendations are built for content managers, SEO specialists, and digital marketing teams, who use them to decide whether to refresh, expand, rewrite, monitor, or leave a page unchanged.

The cost of a wrong call. The two failure modes are asymmetric. A false positive — flagging a healthy page for review — wastes a reviewer's time and erodes trust in the tool. A false negative — missing a page that is genuinely declining — lets traffic and engagement erode further before anyone intervenes, and is harder to recover from. That asymmetry is why every recommendation ships with a reason code rather than a bare score: a reviewer needs to see why a page was flagged in order to catch the false positives quickly.

Why not a fixed rule alone? Search visibility, engagement, click-through rate, average position, and content age interact in ways that are difficult to fully capture in a single hand-written rule. A learned model can, in principle, combine these signals more effectively than a fixed threshold — while still keeping a human reviewer in the loop for every action. §3.1 explains precisely how far this project got toward that goal, and where the final system still leans on the simpler rule.

3 Methodology

3.1 Assumptions & scope

The task was initially framed as a ranking problem DECISION-SUPPORT, with Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) proposed as the success metric, since NDCG rewards placing the truly important items near the top of a list rather than merely classifying items in isolation. In practice, the delivered pipeline is narrower and more auditable: a rule-based priority score (0–100) that converts directly into three action tiers, plus a binary classifier trained to reproduce that rule's high_priority label. No NDCG evaluation was ultimately run CAUTION — this is reported as a limitation, not smoothed over (§5). The working target, high_priority, is therefore a proxy defined from the rule (baseline_score ≥ 70), not an outcome observed independently, such as a traffic lift measured after an actual refresh. That distinction is the single most important assumption in this paper and it is revisited directly in Results and Limitations.

3.2 Baseline rule

A transparent, hand-written rule scores every item from 0–100 using three binary signals:

Table 3 — Baseline rule composition
SignalDefinitionWeight
Staledays_since_last_update ≥ 18040
Visibleimpressions_90d ≥ 50030
Low CTRctr < 130

baseline_score = 40×stale + 30×visible + 30×low_ctr. The score maps to reason codes — STALE_LOWCTR, STALE_VISIBLE, LOWCTR_VISIBLE, or a default REVIEW_PRIORITY — so every ranked item carries a human-readable explanation, not just a number.

Consistency note A later stage of this project (the action playbook) uses a second, slightly different reason-code vocabulary (Healthy, Stale Content, Low CTR, Stale + High Impressions + Low CTR) rather than the set above. Both describe the same underlying score, but a reviewer-facing product should standardize on one vocabulary before shipping — noted here as a concrete cleanup item rather than silently merged.

3.3 Model & validation design

A RandomForestClassifier (100 trees, random_state=42) is trained on three features — days_since_last_update, impressions_90d, and ctr — to predict high_priority. Training the model on the same inputs the rule itself uses lets the two approaches be compared apples-to-apples, using an 80/20 split with a fixed random seed.

Because multiple content items in this dataset share a client_id, a plain random split risks letting information about a client's typical behavior leak between the training and test sets. The model is therefore evaluated twice: once under a standard random 80/20 split, and again under a client-grouped split (GroupShuffleSplit on client_id), which guarantees no client appears in both partitions.

4.2 Why the accuracy is 1.00 — and why that isn't the headline finding

Circularity, not leakage from the future high_priority was defined as baseline_score ≥ 70, and baseline_score is a deterministic function of the exact three columns used as the model's only features. A perfect classifier is therefore the expected outcome, not evidence of unusual predictive skill — the Random Forest is recovering a fixed arithmetic rule from its own inputs, not predicting an independent future event. This is circularity, distinct from temporal leakage; it is disclosed here in full rather than presented as a headline result CAUTION.

Feature importances remain informative under this caveat, because they show the model correctly recovered the relative weighting baked into the rule, without being told the weights explicitly:

5 Limitations & Honest Framing

This analysis identifies observed patterns, not causal relationships. A high refresh score does not guarantee that updating a page will improve its performance. The results are decision support for a human reviewer — never an automated publishing, deletion, or metadata-change trigger. This work does not identify Google ranking factors, does not predict future search performance, and does not establish that refreshing content causes a performance gain.

  • Target circularity — the headline accuracy in §4 measures rule-recovery, not prediction of an independent outcome (§4.2).
  • Scope narrowed from the original framing — the plan proposed a ranking model evaluated with NDCG; the delivered system is a rule plus a classifier over that rule (§3.1).
  • Two data assets, never reconciled — the data-contract audit covers the raw daily warehouse table; all modeling runs on a separate anonymized snapshot (§2.1).
  • The data contract's own grain claim was inaccurate and had to be corrected against the schema; a duplicate-key issue in the raw warehouse table was found and not yet resolved (§2.3).
  • Single snapshot, no time dimension — the modeling dataset is a point-in-time extract; seasonal or temporary effects cannot be separated from durable ones.
  • Missing values in fields such as word_count were observed in the raw data but not imputed or modeled explicitly.
  • Reason-code vocabularies are inconsistent between the two output stages (§3.2) and should be unified before this becomes a shipped tool.

Claim rewrite exercise

As a discipline check, the boldest claim this project could make was deliberately rewritten into safer language:

Before → after Before: "The Random Forest model accurately predicts high-priority content."
After: "The Random Forest model reproduces the baseline scoring rule with high fidelity on this dataset and may help operationalize that rule at scale; it should be used for decision support, not as an independent predictor of future content performance."

6.3 Human review checklist

Before acting on any recommendation, a reviewer confirms:

  • Search intent still matches the page's topic.
  • Content is factually accurate and current.
  • Recent business priorities are reflected in the decision.
  • Important pages are not removed or deprioritized by accident.

No-go list — never automated

  • Publishing new content
  • Deleting pages
  • Rewriting content
  • Changing SEO metadata
  • Making business decisions without human approval

6.4 Monitoring / retrain triggers

The scoring system should be revisited if any of the following occur:

  • CTR shifts substantially across the portfolio.
  • Search rankings move materially.
  • New content types are introduced that the rule wasn't designed for.
  • Performance on fresh data degrades.
  • Business priorities change.
